    COVID-19 update: Kenya records 4 new deaths, 64 more infections

    - The government has expressed optimism the second wave of COVID-19 infections is subsiding - Government Spokesperson Cyrus Oguna, however, urged Kenyans to exercise extra caution as schools reopen next week

    14 Aug 2024, 00:00

    - The government has expressed optimism the second wave of COVID-19 infections is subsiding

    - Government Spokesperson Cyrus Oguna, however, urged Kenyans to exercise extra caution as schools reopen next week

    - Health CS Kagwe said the country expects its first doses of COVID-19 to be delivered in the first half of 2020

    - The WHO Thursday approved the Pfizer vaccine for emergency use shining more hope on crashing the pandemic

    Kenya's tally of confirmed COVID-19 cases on Saturday, January 2, increased by 64 to 96,678, Ministry of Health announced.

    While announcing the new infections, Health Cabinet Secretary Mutahi Kagwe said among those who tested positive were 35 males and 26 females.

    They were picked from 1,852 samples tested over the past 24 hours.

    Out of the cases, 59 were Kenyans while five were foreigners out of which the eldest was 81 years while the youngest was six.

    On recoveries, Kagwe disclosed some 55 patients had been discharged, 41 being from the Home-Based Care Program while 14 were from hospitals.

    The latest discharges pushed to 79,012, the number of recoveries Kenya has recorded since April 1, 2020.

    The country's fatalities grew to 1,685 after the disease claimed the lives of four.

    The new cases were announced Saturday as the government expressed optimism the second phase of infections that struck the country at around October was easing out.

    Through a brief statement, Government Spokesperson Cyrus Oguna said despite indications the pandemic was "dissipating", extra caution needed to be taken especially at this time when schools were expected to reopen on January 4.

    For about two weeks now, Kenya has been recording a lower number of daily infections suggesting the curve is flattening.

    "As we continue to be hopeful that the emergency phase of the coronavirus pandemic dissipates, schools re-opening constitutes part of the wider ‘recovery’ strategy by the government to regain lost school time to protect our children as well as put the economy back on track to where it was in the pre COVID-19 pandemic era," Oguna said.

    In a separate story, TUKO.co.ke reported that Health CS Kagwe revealed the government had ordered vaccines which were expected in the country within the first half of this year.

    Health care workers, Kagwe said, will be given priority during deployment of the vaccines.

    The CS, however, did not disclose which dose the country was expecting but experts intimate Kenya will most likely go for the Oxford University/AstraZeneca dose which is low-cost, easy to store and is also being tested locally.

    TUKO.co.ke also reported WHO on Thursday approved the Pfizer vaccine for emergency use.

    WHO's approval unlocks the vaccine to other countries which had not authorised the dose for deployment.

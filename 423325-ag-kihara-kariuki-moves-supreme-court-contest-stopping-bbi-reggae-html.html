    Attorney General Paul Kihara Kariuki is heading to the Supreme Court to challenge the appellate court's decision that stopped the Building Bridges Initiative (BBI) process.

    A seven-judge Court of Appeal bench on Friday, August 20, found the process unconstitutional and handed the promoters a blow that could potentially stop the sounds and drumbeats of BBI reggae.

    However, in a draft document, Kariuki identified three major grounds that he expects to anchor his appeal in the Lady Justice Martha Koome-led court.

    They include the application of the basic structure doctrine, remit of constitutional amendments by popular initiative and presidential immunity.

    On the basic structure doctrine, the government's principal legal advisor argued that the Court of Appeal and the High Court judges overstretched their mandate in their ruling.

    He insisted that historically, the doctrine had applied in situations where amendment power was reserved to parliament and where the people had no direct involvement through a referendum.

    The AG further faulted the Justice Daniel Musinga-led bench, saying they missed the point when they listed President Uhuru Kenyatta as the promoter of the BBI popular initiative to amend the constitution.

    He exuded confidence that the apex court will overturn the decision and give a green light for the constitutional amendment process through the BBI to continue.

    Court of Appeal's judgment

    President of the Court of Appeal Musinga and judge Patrick Kiage, in their lengthy submissions, agreed with the bench that the president's role is limited to protecting and defending the constitution.

    On his part, Kiage argued that although the president enjoys certain powers as provided for in the constitution, he does not shoulder any obligation to initiate constitutional changes.

    According to the bench, the structure of the constitution can only be altered through civic education, public participation, assembly debates preceding a referendum.

    Whether the Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission (IEBC) was properly constituted at the time of the BBI process and if it acted in line with the statutory requirements, the court stated the IEBC must carry out nationwide voter registration before a proposed referendum and a continuous voter registration in all constituencies.
